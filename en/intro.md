# Introduction

Frederick Engels (1873–1886)

First Published: in Russian and German in the USSR in 1925,  
except for *Part Played by Labour*, 1896 and *Natural Science and the Spirit World*, 1898.

---

## 1. The Place of Dialectics in Natural Science

Modern natural science has inherited from the past a certain mode of thought – the metaphysical method – which, in the main, hindered the development of a correct understanding of nature. For the metaphysician, things and their mental images, concepts, are fixed, rigid, given objects of investigation, to be considered one after the other and independently of each other.

But when we consider nature, or the history of mankind, or our own intellectual activity, the first picture presented to us is that of an endless web of interconnections and interactions, in which nothing remains what, where, or as it was. This is the dialectical mode of thought, which alone can do justice to the totality of nature’s phenomena.

## 2. The Limits of the Mechanical Conception of Nature

The mechanical conception of nature, which was carried to its extreme by the natural science of the 18th century, explained all changes in nature as merely quantitative changes of pre-existing mechanical motion. But qualitative changes, transformations of one form of motion into another, cannot be reduced to mere mechanics.

Heat, light, electricity, magnetism – these are not forms of motion of ponderable matter, but forms of motion of matter in general. To understand them, we must go beyond the mechanical standpoint and recognise the qualitative diversity of motion.
